Highlights of the AiXin Educational Aids Program in Shitai County

             (English version edited and abridged from Chinese text)

 

                By Yongkai Weng and Chuanshuo Hu, AiXin Foundation volunteers

           

In order to monitor the use of the educational assistance funds donated by Dr. Zaishen Shen and Ms. Peizhen Shen, we made a fact-finding trip to Shitai County, Anhui Province on October 3 and 4, 2006.  The following is a summary report of the trip and some of our observations. 

 

Shitai County is located deep inside the mountain region of southern Anhui, wedged between the popular tourist area of Huang Shan and the famous Buddhist holy land of Jiuhua Shan.  It is a scenic place surrounded by verdant hills and crystal clear waters.  There are 814 administrative villages within the county boundaries.  With a population of 110,000 and a total land area of 1,403 square kilometers, the county has a land cultivation of 0.5 hectare* per capita.  Among its 110,000 inhabitants, more than 10,000 have to find work outside the county.  The county reports a per-capita income of RMB1,494 per year, while the poverty income line for the county is RMB600.  The local economy is supported by a relative abundance of processed tea leaves and bamboo byproducts.  In addition, villagers also rely on the planting of hot pepper and the gathering of red mountain berries (medicinal cornel), sold to the traditional herb medicine industry, to bolster income.  However, because of a lack of transportation facilities and a rather primitive commercial information system, the county’s economy remains severely underdeveloped.  At present, about 30 percent of its residents live below the official poverty line.  The county is officially declared by the government as an area of poverty and economic hardship.

 

            Due to the poor workplace safety conditions and inadequate healthcare facilities, aggravated in no small measure by the local custom of marrying close relatives, there are a sizable number of disabled people in the county--more than 5,700 with some type of deformity or disability, about 5.5 percent of the total county population.  Of this number, 267 are children and young adolescents. 

 

            The target group for our home visits consists of young students with disabilities or healthy students living in families headed by a disabled person.  These children are at their prime age of learning but forced to quit school because of disabilities--either of their own or of someone in the family.  It is sad to see them suffer from the cruelty of life at such a tender age.  The local government and the county’s Association of Disabled Persons (a semi-official organization, hereinafter referred to as the ADP) have compiled a large amount of statistical information related to the disabled residents.  On this trip, the ADP selected 111 among the 141 neediest students affected by disabilities as the subject of our observation.

Our home visits were mostly in the villages of Jitan and Qijing (photos attached).  These visits gave us a lot of information of which we were unaware before.  We now realize that, even with the waiving of tuition and incidental expenses for the “poverty affected” families, students still have to pay for supplemental instruction material, the amount of which varies by locality but generally ranges from several tens RMB to as much as three hundred RMB.  In many areas, schools with relatively small enrollments are forced to merge in order to economize on the use of available resources.  As a result of fewer schools, some students now have to travel longer distances to get to the school, and some may find it necessary to live at the school during the week.  This situation has raised the amount of expenses even further.  It is estimated that room and board cost between RMB800 and RMB1,000 per student per year.  Given that most families have to provide for two to three students, the total amount could be quite a burden.  The situation is exacerbated further when disabilities reduce the number of able bodied wage earners in the family.

 

            From these home visits and from consultations with the ADP staff, we decided to allocate the RMB100,000 donated by Dr. Shen and his family over a period of two to three years for the financial aid program, focusing for the time being on students with disabilities themselves or having family members with serious disabilities.  The criteria set up for the financial aid were: (1) severe poverty in the family; (2) an indication of self-motivation on the part of the student in pursuing school work; (3) a strong will of support on the part of the family for the student’s continued education, and (4) the family’s ability to use the financial aid effectively.  The amount of aid was decided to be in the neighborhood of US$100 per student per year, with the additional condition that the use of the money be closely monitored and subject to occasional spot checks.  In order to guarantee that the aid money is used only for the student’s educational needs and room and board expenses, the ADP will help to establish a specific bank credit account for each student at the school.  Any withdrawal from the account must be co-signed by the student, his/her parent or guardian, and his/her teacher.

 

            The home visits clearly showed the importance of strong, active support from local government agencies and civic organizations.  Such local support enables social aid workers and charity volunteers to provide relief services much more effectively.  In Shitai County, for instance, the substantial amount of preparatory work and background information provided by the local government and the ADP allowed us to identify the neediest students and to distribute the aid money quickly.  Undoubtedly, we need to maintain a two-way channel of communication between the local authorities and the donor community in the U.S.  In doing so, we help to maximize the benefits of the donated funds for those school children most in need of help.

 

            Although on this trip we were able to provide financial aid only to a portion of the 111 poorest disabled students selected, we believe that we have laid a solid foundation for our future work, and have refocused the attention of the donor community back home to the dire situation children affected by disabilities face.  It is our hope that overseas Chinese, especially those who are originally from the province of Anhui, would contact us through various channels and offer their generous help.

 

            A couple of interesting episodes occurred on the trip.  Although Dr. Shen and his family donated the estate left by their parents, they actually knew few specific details of their ancestors’ family and personal lives.  The county official familiar with residents’ genealogy records, Deputy County Administrator Xixiang Wu, took the trouble of locating the historical records of the only four Shen families in Shitai that were known to have held high government positions.  He then had these records digitally photographed so they could be sent to the Shens in the United States by email.  We were very impressed by Mr. Wu’s diligence and attention to our donors’ interests.

 

            Another interesting episode was that we ran into a fellow student who was sent to the countryside during the Cultural Revolution and was in the same farm labor brigade with Yongkai more than 30 years ago.  Her name is Gendi Lu, now the Administrative Assistant of the Qijin Village.  Although Gendi had been ill for several days and was still on medication when we arrived, she insisted on accompanying us in our family visits.  She felt her familiarity with local residents’ family situations would make our fact-finding work easier and more accurate.  It was really amazing that the fellowship forged some 30 years ago, during that tumultuous era in the contemporary Chinese history, got revisited so unexpectedly.

 

            In closing, we would like to thank Mr. Heng Hu, president of the Association for the Promotion of International Cooperation of Anhui Province, who coordinated among our local contacts and planned the entire itinerary for us, and used his own vacation time during the National Anniversary holidays to accompany us everyday.  Our thanks also go to Mr. Shiyou Li, president of the local ADP, and Mr. Xie, director of the local Workshop for the Disabled, both of whom accompanied us during our entire trip.  Without their help, our trip to Shitai County would not have been so smooth and fruitful.  We conclude this report with our heart-felt gratitude for their assistance, and with the newly gained confidence for the successful completion of the AiXin rural educational aid programs.
