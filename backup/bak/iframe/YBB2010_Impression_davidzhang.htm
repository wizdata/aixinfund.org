Journal for YBB

 

July 31st¡ªI woke up in the morning not knowing what to expect, but I had high hopes for a trip that was entirely designed to bring the world to a small, poor, rural village in the middle of Shandong Province in China. Getting on the bus was a daunting task¡ª8 hours of sitting in a seat. But it turned out to be much, much more fun than I did expect. When the Shanghai tour group came on the bus, I didn¡¯t know most of them. Yet sitting alone for 8 straight hours seemed like a really boring prospect, so I began to play Mafia with the rest of the people on board. In that manner, a few hours passed like a few minutes, and before I knew it, we were turning on to a dusty road in the middle of absolutely nowhere. With sand and dust everywhere, I jumped off the bus into perhaps the hottest temperatures I have experienced in years. Turning around and squinting in the sunlight, the afternoon air and the buildings surrounding me seemed to meld together. One thing led to another, and pretty soon, we were introduced to our dorms. An ominous row of darkened rooms, we had to clean out the musty rooms that were dirtier than my garage. After scrubbing and sweeping, driving away hordes of insects, waiting for a fix on a broken faucet, and finally getting our room in decent condition, we went to dinner at a restaurant not far from where the school is. Getting back sort of late, I took a cold shower and went to sleep on a lovely wooden board.

 

August 1st¡ªAfter a surprisingly comfortable night considering the circumstances I slept under, I discovered we were eating breakfast at the same restaurant as we ate dinner last night. The food was decent, but I was really hoping for some good milk and cereal. I guess not all dreams come true. On the way back to the school, we stopped at a convenience store that looked pretty good. There people bought flashlights, washing bowls, towels, clothes hangers, drinks, anything that one could want (well, maybe not anything).Though I regret the decision now, I chose not to purchase anything at that store. However, we made another stop on the return trip, at an ice cream and popsicle stand by the side of the dusty road filled with puddles of mud. Now, I do enjoy ice cream in America, but the popsicles in China tasted absolutely divine. Either that or I was really thirsty/hot. The rest of the day was spent inside a hot and stuffy room on the third floor of the school, learning about the master plans for the lessons we would teach tomorrow. After a couple hours of fanning and reluctant listening, I finally grasped the enormity of the task ahead of me when the presentations of the master lessons of each class were over and felt totally unprepared for tomorrow. With that thought, the rest of the day passed by quickly. I was surprised by how quickly sleep washed over me that night.

 

August 2nd¡ªThe first day of teaching started off a little differently than usual. Still getting accustomed to the regimen, I woke up groggily and prepared myself for the day, then went to the cafeteria for breakfast that consisted of eggs, carrots, and tasteless flatbread. I did not know why, but I had first day jitters just thinking about the prospect of an entire classroom staring at me while I was teaching. I strolled into Classroom 5 right after breakfast and immediately tripped. Not the best first impression. At least the class laughed. After introducing ourselves (Me, Danting, Peter, Qian Peng, Wang Bing, and Xu Han Yue) began the first class of the day, English. For English, Danting and I were the lead teachers for the class. We decided to strictly adhere to the original lesson plan for minimal hitches during the class, but soon, both expected and unexpected problems occurred. Expectedly, the language barrier became an issue. Only after a few sentences in Chinese, words appeared that became difficult to translate. I burned with disappointment inwardly, but then the college students stepped in. While Danting and I were busy with our recovery, the college students filled in the time with a quick overview of the material we had covered already. At that moment, I realized that cohesive teamwork was going to be a cinch for us. Now the unexpected problem¡ªthe students were really smart. They already knew the vocabulary and grammar we were trying to teach them. With no apparent solution in mind, we decided to move on. However, this problem became a recurring issue throughout the day. Whether it was music, computer, or travel around the world, my students knew the material already. I was both proud and disheartened, and finally experienced for myself the differences between the education system in America and China. After lunch and a meeting on the fourth floor for the entire school, it was time for project. Danting, Peter, and I had no idea about what the project was but the college students told us it was a project that they had been working on prior to this trip. I watched with curiosity as Wang Bing led them in a game that translated to Squirrel Hugging a Big Tree. At the end of the game, the students, huffing and puffing, listened as the college students capped the game off with a quick lesson on teamwork and cooperation, the way to win the game. I realized that our college students¡¯ project was on teamwork and capacity building just as it was time for the last lesson of the day: P.E. The minute I told the kids that we were to go outside on the field, 50 screaming children charged the door. As the entire school streamed outwards, organization was hopeless and so we allowed our students to do whichever activity they wanted. When it was finally dismissal, I was deafened by the silence that greeted my ears. Wearily, I trudged back to the lounge and was happy to find that I had free time. I spent it playing a game called Signs with the other volunteers. After dinner, I sat through a meeting in the hot stuffy science room on the third floor to cover today¡¯s events. My group was praised by Mrs. Teng on our teamwork. I found out that every group encountered the same problem we did, that the students knew the material already. It soon became a blur to me as I got drowsier and drowsier. As soon as the meeting ended, I rushed out the door to go take a cold shower that cleared up my senses. The long day ended as I fell asleep in the stuffiness of the dorm room.

 

August 3rd¡ªIn comparison to yesterday, today was a breeze. The main event during today¡¯s class time was me getting to know the students better. Yesterday, I felt myself distanced from them, but I knew that I should probably socialize with at least some of them. One thing I noticed about the students was that they were really shy and were not very willing to introduce themselves to me. But there were exceptions. One boy in particular, whose English name was Max, seemed to stir up trouble in the classroom effortlessly. I will never forget his mischievous, cheeky complexion. With inwardly slanted eyebrows, every time I saw him I felt as if he was going to pull the hair of the girl in front of him, or throw a spitball at me. When I went over to ask him to introduce himself, he muttered his name and, with a cheeky smile, ducked away. I sighed and went to another row of students. In this manner, I met many of the students¡ªwho were very interesting people. There was Sasi, the level 8 Gu Zheng player. There was Austin, a gentle giant who was an expert at Chinese Chess. There was Amelia, a tough girl who wanted to play basketball with all the guys. And there was Joe, the boy with the loudest voice I had ever heard who was very prone to fighting. I realized that I would have a hard time planning the final performance if the students were shy even talking to me. Pondering this question, the day passed effortlessly¡ªtoo effortlessly. It was after school had ended that I realized I had left my laptop and backpack in the classroom, but now it was locked and too late. Sighing, I waited anxiously for tomorrow and hope that my belongings are still there.

At any rate, I decided not to wash my clothes then and wash it tonight. Blinking the sleep from my eyes, I stepped outside and was really surprised to find it was 6:46 already. After washing up and getting ready, I remembered that I had left my name tag in the meeting room last night, lost my umbrella, and left my backpack and laptop inside the classroom. Standing in the rain for a few minutes and feeling flustered, I decided to sit on the problems. The rest of the time went by quickly, and soon it was time to start class. I strolled in the classroom with a nonchalant air, and was happy to find that my stuff was still there. Pretty soon, most of the class arrived. I guessed that the empty seats were due to the heavy rain, but I also saw some unfamiliar faces that I didn¡¯t recognize. When Bing arrived, we started off English. After a slight review of vocabulary that the students knew very well, Bing taught them a poem I thought was really romantic:

At that point, our skit was ready, but we decided to save that for last and so we taught them a song (I¡¯m a Little Teapot). Learning the song was tricky, but it was finally time for the skit. Skitting the story of Cinderella seemed pointless to me, but seeing the amusement on the faces and hearing it in the laughter of the children gave me renewed motivation and enthusiasm. In the skit, I was Cinderella and Danting was the prince. The entire play was a comical reenactment and the students laughed throughout the skit. Glad that this was such a success, I asked for volunteers to perform a skit of their own. To my surprise, silence greeted me. The fifty laughing students had suddenly turned solemn at the hint that they might perform. Though in the end, I picked out a few students to do the skit, the heart had gone out from it and I was again left speculating about the final performance. The day took its toll on me and the heat became unbearable. That night, I went to sleep without washing my clothes because I was too tired.

 

August 5th¡ªRemembering my promise to myself, I finally faced the large pile of unwashed clothes that had subtly built up under my nose. With some free time after lunch, I headed off to the dorms and dutifully picked up the dirty laundry, then set a shirt in a washing bowl. Filling it up with water, I added some laundry detergent and¡ªI had no idea what to do next. I had never washed my own clothes before. So after minutes of random scrubbing, I hoped I had made it cleaner than it was before. I hung it up on the clothesline outside, and rinsed and repeated. In this manner I plowed through the pile until I had taken up about half the clothesline. But at least I was finally done. With dripping hands, I watched with satisfaction as my hand-washed clothes began drying. School today was uneventful, but dinner today was special. We had the chance to make our own food¡ªwe would wrap dumplings. I pondered whether or not to go to the cafeteria for making the dumplings. On the one hand, I could get to experience firsthand the joys of working in a kitchen. On the other hand, the kitchen was sweltering and I would be sweating buckets. I chose the former. At any rate, when I arrived, the two tables had already filled up with eager volunteers watching two women, one at each table, skillfully knead the dough into many small spheres, and then flatten them with the rolling pin into a circular shape. As I edged into one of the tables, the woman grabbed the spoon and put a dollop of the mixture of pork and vegetables into the center of the dumpling skin, then deftly folded up the dumpling and pinched the skin so that it remained shut. She made it look so easy, I knew I had to try to make one. As we gathered around the table, she made sure that everyone had thoroughly washed their hands. Then, we each picked up a circular piece of dumpling skin and prepared to make our own dinner. When it was my turn to put the pork-vegetable mixture into the dumpling skin, I spooned up too much and my folding attempt was unsuccessful. Sighing, I produced a misshapen monstrosity that had the mixture leaking out of the sides. The next one I tried was better, but I had scooped up too little this time. Not wanting to create another weird dumpling, I decided to make it into something better¡ªa mini steamed bun. I pinched the sides upward and twisted the top of it until I had made the perfect shape, then placed my masterpiece onto the communal tray. And then I set about my work methodically, taking great care when scooping up the mixture into the dumpling. 20 minutes later, there was no more of the pork and vegetable mixture and we called it quits. I gazed upon the tray¡ªa sampling of the most unique shapes of dumpling one could ever see. I watched proudly as the women carried the tray into the kitchen. As cooking the massive batch of dumplings would take time, I headed off to the A/C room. When dinner time came along, I rushed into the cafeteria to see the fruits of our labor. The women came out with big, steaming bowls of dumplings and everyone dug in. Combined with the sauce that was put out, it was the most delicious meal I had ever had in Shandong. With a full stomach of dumplings, I was contented for the rest of the day and went to sleep happy.

As dinner reverted back to the regular cuisine (flatbread, a mild soup, vegetables, and another constantly changing side dish) perhaps due to the food issues, I began to feel almost bored with the monotonous routing that was settling in. August 9th, 10th, and 11th¡ªThe weekend vacation, in my opinion, was far from relaxing. But I felt that it was still a welcome break from the now everyday task of teaching. As an added bonus, the motel seemed luxurious with its beds, showers and toilets. But now that it's over, I plunge headlong into the second week of YBB. Despite having called the dumplings the most delicious meal in Shandong, I now renounce my statement and state that the Shandong Jian Bing, or more colloquially the burritos among the volunteers, are the best thing I have eaten here. I never could imagine that bean sprouts, carrots, and other vegetables, along with a sausage, sweet sauce, and spicy powder all wrapped up in a tough, stringy flatbread could taste so good. I had discovered this small nugget of food treasure on a store across the street. However, I never knew the hidden potential risks. Only hours later, I discovered that many people had become sick from eating these delicious, but actually dirty, burritos. The parent chaperones and Mrs. Teng were very displeased when they discovered we had purchased food from outside of the school. Then, to my surprise, the teachers who owned the popsicle store announced that they were going to start a burrito stand. And so whenever I would be sick of eating in the cafeteria, it was the burrito stand to my rescue. These three days passed quickly, with the teaching inside the classroom going very smoothly.

The day was allotted as the first half dedicated to a rehearsal, then the second half the actual performance. Even I teared up, reflecting on how much I would miss the family I had here, in Shandong Linyi in a place called Square City, on the other side of the world from America.
